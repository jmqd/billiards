# Swerve notes

Working notes distilled from the local whitepapers on cue-ball swerve / curve.

## Summary

- Swerve increases with **cue elevation** and the amount of sidespin.
- Swerve occurs with practically all English shots because the cue must usually be elevated at least a little to clear the rails.
- Swerve is **delayed with faster shot speed**.
- Swerve occurs **only while the cue ball is sliding**; once rolling begins, the cue ball heads in a straight line tangent to the earlier path.
- Swerve occurs **earlier with sticky cloth** and later on slick cloth.
- Swerve occurs **earlier with a follow shot** than with a draw shot.
- Swerve angle is **larger with a draw shot** than with a follow shot.
- Swerve angle can be predicted / visualized with the **Coriolis massé-shot aiming method**.

## Notes from local sources

### `whitepapers/got_english.pdf`
This is the cleanest local summary source. Its listed principles state, in substance:

- swerve increases with cue elevation and amount of sidespin;
- swerve occurs with practically all sidespin shots because the cue must be elevated to clear the rails;
- swerve occurs only while the CB is sliding, and once rolling begins the CB heads in a straight line;
- swerve occurs earlier with sticky cloth and later on slick cloth;
- swerve occurs earlier with follow than draw;
- swerve angle is larger with draw than follow;
- the Coriolis massé-shot aiming system can be used to predict / visualize swerve.

### `whitepapers/squirt_swerve_and_throw_wrap_up.pdf`
This note reinforces that:

- cue-ball swerve depends on cue elevation, shot speed, distance, and table conditions;
- faster shots delay how much swerve has time to develop before object-ball impact;
- short shots often show less completed swerve simply because there is less time / distance for it to take effect.

### `whitepapers/tp_a_4_post_impact_cue_ball_trajectory_for_any_cut_angle_speed_and_spin.pdf`
This technical proof is important for the motion-phase picture:

- the cue ball's curved path applies **only while it is sliding**;
- once sliding ceases, the cue ball moves in a **straight line tangent** to the earlier curve;
- the paper models the curved segment as a sliding-phase effect with fixed friction direction during that interval.

### `whitepapers/coriolis_was_brilliant_but_he_didn_t_have_a_high_speed_camera_part_v_masse_shot_aiming.pdf`
This note ties the visualization / aiming language back to Coriolis:

- the Coriolis / "BAR" massé aiming system helps visualize the **final cue-ball direction**;
- shot speed, cue elevation, and table conditions affect the actual path shape;
- sticky / high-friction cloth increases massé action.

## Practical interpretation

For ordinary English shots with a slightly elevated cue, the cue ball does not instantly take its full curved path. The curve develops during the **sliding** portion of the shot, with:

- more cue elevation -> more swerve,
- more available time / distance -> more completed swerve,
- faster speed -> later / less completed swerve over a fixed distance,
- draw tending to produce a larger eventual swerve angle than follow.
